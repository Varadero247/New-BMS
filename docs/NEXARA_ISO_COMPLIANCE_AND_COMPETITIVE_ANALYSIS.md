### 6.1 Standards Coverage Matrix — Full Detail

Nexara IMS is the only enterprise platform on the market that delivers native, clause-mapped compliance coverage across more than twenty internationally recognised management system standards within a single unified architecture. Rather than offering isolated point solutions that must be stitched together through fragile integrations, Nexara embeds each standard's requirements directly into its data models, workflow engines, and reporting structures, enabling organisations to simultaneously maintain and evidence conformance across an entire standards portfolio.

**Quality Management Cluster.** At the foundation of Nexara's standards architecture sits the quality cluster, led by ISO 9001:2015 — the world's most widely adopted management system standard. Built on 44 Prisma data models and covering all ten clauses end-to-end, the quality module is complemented by three sector-specific extensions: IATF 16949:2016 for automotive manufacturers, AS9100D for aerospace and defence suppliers, and ISO 13485:2016 for medical device organisations operating under FDA 21 CFR Part 820 and EU MDR requirements. Each extension inherits the core ISO 9001 framework and layers sector-specific controls — Advanced Product Quality Planning (APQP), Core Tools, Design History Files, and Device Master Records — directly on top.

**Environmental, Safety, and Health Cluster.** ISO 14001:2015 (Environmental Management) and ISO 45001:2018 (Occupational Health and Safety) are implemented as distinct but tightly integrated modules. Environmental aspects and impacts, legal registers, objectives, and CAPA workflows are cross-referenced with health and safety risk assessments, incident management, and permit-to-work controls. ISO 50001:2018 (Energy Management) extends the environmental cluster with dedicated energy baselines, EnPIs, and SEUs, supporting mandatory ESOS compliance in the UK.

**Food Safety Cluster.** ISO 22000:2018 is implemented with full HACCP plan management, prerequisite programmes, hazard analysis, and critical control point monitoring. The module satisfies requirements for BRCGS, SQF, and FSSC 22000 certification audits.

**Information Security and Governance Cluster.** ISO 27001:2022 is implemented with all 93 Annex A controls mapped across four domains — Organisational, People, Physical, and Technological. This is complemented by ISO 42001:2023 (AI Management Systems) and ISO 37001:2016 (Anti-Bribery Management Systems), positioning Nexara as the only IMS platform to natively address artificial intelligence governance and anti-corruption requirements.

**Risk and Asset Management.** ISO 31000:2018 provides the cross-cutting risk framework that underpins every module. ISO 55001:2014 (Asset Management) is served by the CMMS module with full asset lifecycle tracking. ISO 22301:2019 (Business Continuity) is addressed through emergency management, incident response, and business impact analysis workflows.

**Regulatory and ESG Cluster.** GDPR, UK POPIA, and the CDM Regulations are embedded in the platform's data governance, consent management, and permit-to-work modules respectively. ESG reporting aligns to GRI Standards, TCFD recommendations, ISO 26000 social responsibility guidance, and the UK Corporate Governance Code, with structured outputs for Streamlined Energy and Carbon Reporting (SECR) and mandatory climate disclosures.

This breadth of standards coverage — twenty-two discrete standards and regulatory frameworks across eight thematic clusters — enables organisations to pursue multi-standard certification programmes using a single system of record, eliminating the duplication of effort and evidence that characterises multi-vendor compliance estates.

### 6.2 ISO 9001:2015 — Clause-by-Clause Coverage

ISO 9001:2015 is the global benchmark for quality management and the certification most commonly required by procurement bodies, regulated industries, and export markets. Nexara's quality management module delivers verified, auditable coverage of all ten clauses through purpose-built data structures, automated workflows, and real-time performance dashboards.

**Clauses 1–3: Scope, Normative References, and Terms and Definitions.** Nexara's platform scope configuration allows organisations to define their certification boundary, exclusions, and documented scope statement within the system of record. Context factors and terms are managed through the internal glossary module, with version-controlled definitions accessible to all users.

**Clause 4: Context of the Organisation.** Clause 4.1 (Understanding the organisation and its context) is addressed through the Issues Register, which captures internal and external issues using a structured SWOT and PESTLE framework. Each issue is categorised, assigned an owner, and linked to risks and objectives, providing the evidence trail required during certification audits. Clause 4.2 (Understanding the needs and expectations of interested parties) is served by the Interested Parties Register — a dedicated module where stakeholders are categorised (customer, regulator, employee, supplier, community), their relevant requirements documented, and monitoring actions assigned. Clause 4.3 (Determining the scope) is managed through the Scope Management module, which records inclusions, exclusions, and their justifications. Clause 4.4 (Quality management system and its processes) is fully supported by the Process Map module, where each business process is documented with inputs, outputs, sequence, interaction points, responsible owners, associated risks, and performance metrics — satisfying the documented process requirement without prescribing a bureaucratic approach.

**Clause 5: Leadership.** Clause 5.1 (Leadership and commitment) is evidenced through management review records, signed-off quality policy documents, and objective approval workflows that require top management authorisation. Clause 5.2 (Policy) delivers a version-controlled Quality Policy module with acknowledgement tracking, ensuring all personnel have been made aware of and understand the policy. Clause 5.3 (Organisational roles, responsibilities and authorities) is managed through the role assignment and delegation features of the HR and RBAC modules, with organisational charts and responsibility matrices exportable for audit packs.

**Clause 6: Planning.** Clause 6.1 (Actions to address risks and opportunities) is the central concern of the Quality Risk Register, providing likelihood-consequence matrices, risk heat maps, and linked action plans. Risks and opportunities are managed in a single register with a configurable scoring methodology. Clause 6.2 (Quality objectives and planning to achieve them) is served by the Objectives module, where SMART objectives are created, assigned owners and target dates, linked to KPIs, and tracked through milestone completion. Clause 6.3 (Planning of changes) is managed through the Change Management module, with structured change requests, impact assessments, and approval workflows.

**Clause 7: Support.** Clause 7.1 (Resources) covers infrastructure, measurement resources, and organisational knowledge. The Calibration module manages measurement equipment with calibration schedules, certificates, and overdue alerts (7.1.5). The Asset module tracks infrastructure assets with maintenance schedules. Clause 7.2 (Competence) is addressed by the Competence Register, linking personnel to required competencies and evidencing training completion against each. Clause 7.3 (Awareness) is supported by the Training module with acknowledgement records. Clause 7.4 (Communication) is managed through the Notifications and Communication Planning modules. Clause 7.5 (Documented information) is fully addressed by the Document Control module with version history, approval workflows, review cycles, controlled distribution, and external document registers. Clause 7.6 is not a clause in ISO 9001:2015 (the standard moved from the 2008 structure); the platform's controlled document module covers all 7.5 sub-clauses.

**Clause 8: Operation.** Clause 8.1 (Operational planning and control) is supported by the process documentation and change management modules. Clause 8.2 (Requirements for products and services) captures customer requirements, review records, and contract review logs. Clause 8.3 (Design and development) provides a full design project module with planning stages, inputs, outputs, review records, verification, validation, and design change control. Clause 8.4 (Control of externally provided processes) is served by the Supplier Management module with approved supplier lists, evaluation criteria, performance scorecards, and re-approval workflows. Clause 8.5 (Production and service provision) records production controls, traceability identifications, customer property records, and preservation logs. Clause 8.6 (Release of products and services) captures inspection and test records with acceptance authority sign-offs. Clause 8.7 (Control of nonconforming outputs) is the NCR module — arguably the most used module in a quality system — with full CAPA linkage, containment records, and disposition tracking.

**Clause 9: Performance Evaluation.** Clause 9.1 (Monitoring, measurement, analysis and evaluation) is addressed by the KPI Dashboard, customer satisfaction survey module, and data analysis reports. Customer satisfaction (9.1.2) is delivered through the built-in survey tool with external respondent links. Analysis and evaluation (9.1.3) is supported by trend charts, SPC data, and AI-assisted pattern recognition. Clause 9.2 (Internal audit) provides the full audit programme management module with audit scheduling, checklists, findings, corrective action linkage, and audit report generation. Clause 9.3 (Management review) delivers a structured management review module with configurable agenda templates, input data aggregation from all modules, minutes capture, and action tracking.

**Clause 10: Improvement.** Clause 10.1 (General) is addressed through the continual improvement dashboard showing trend data, objective progress, and closed loop performance. Clause 10.2 (Nonconformity and corrective action) is the CAPA module — with five-step methodology support (identification, containment, root cause analysis, corrective action, verification of effectiveness). Root cause analysis supports multiple methods: 5-Why, fishbone, fault tree, and free-text. Clause 10.3 (Continual improvement) is evidenced through the Opportunities Register and the innovation pipeline, where improvement ideas are captured, prioritised, resourced, and tracked to completion.

### 6.3 ISO 14001:2015 — Clause-by-Clause Coverage

ISO 14001:2015 sets the internationally recognised framework for environmental management systems and is increasingly mandated by major procurement bodies, public sector clients, and supply chain sustainability requirements. Nexara's Environmental Management module delivers comprehensive clause-by-clause coverage through a purpose-built architecture aligned to the standard's Annex SL high-level structure.

**Clauses 1–3: Scope, Normative References, Terms and Definitions.** The platform's scope configuration allows environmental management system boundaries to be defined separately from the quality or H&S scope, reflecting the modular certification approach common in multi-standard organisations. Environmental-specific terminology is managed in the integrated glossary with version-controlled definitions.

**Clause 4: Context of the Organisation.** Clause 4.1 (Understanding the organisation and its context) maps internal and external environmental issues using a structured PESTLE-based issue register, capturing economic, environmental, regulatory, technological, and societal factors affecting the organisation's environmental obligations. Clause 4.2 (Understanding the needs and expectations of interested parties) maintains a dedicated environmental interested parties register, distinguishing between parties with legal and binding requirements and those with broader expectations — a critical distinction for determining which requirements must be addressed in the EMS. Clause 4.3 (Determining the scope) documents EMS boundaries with audit-ready justification. Clause 4.4 (Environmental management system) ensures processes are established, maintained, and continuously improved through linked process documentation.

**Clause 5: Leadership.** Clause 5.1 (Leadership and commitment) is evidenced through top management's ownership of environmental policy approval workflows, management review chair records, and resource allocation decisions documented in the system. Clause 5.2 (Environmental policy) provides a version-controlled policy module with acknowledgement tracking, communication records, and public availability controls. Clause 5.3 (Organisational roles, responsibilities and authorities) assigns accountability for all environmental functions through the RBAC system with documented delegation records.

**Clause 6: Planning.** Clause 6.1.1 (Actions to address risks and opportunities related to EMS) provides an environmental risk register with linkage to aspect significance scores and legal compliance requirements. Clause 6.1.2 (Environmental aspects) is the platform's flagship environmental feature: the Aspects and Impacts Register. Each environmental aspect is documented with its associated environmental impact, life cycle phase, affected media, and a significance score calculated using the platform's configurable scoring algorithm — accounting for severity, probability, duration, extent, reversibility, regulatory profile, and stakeholder sensitivity. Aspects scoring above the configurable significance threshold (default: 15 of 28) are automatically flagged as significant and prioritised for control. Clause 6.1.3 (Compliance obligations) maintains the Environmental Legal Register, automatically categorising legislative instruments, conditions of environmental permits, consent agreements, and voluntary commitments. Each obligation is linked to the aspects or activities it applies to, with review frequency controls and compliance assessment records. Clause 6.1.4 (Planning action) creates action plans from risks, aspects, and legal obligations, with owner assignment, target dates, and effectiveness verification. Clause 6.2 (Environmental objectives and planning) delivers SMART environmental objectives with milestone tracking, progress dashboards, and KPI linkage. Each objective traces back to at least one significant aspect and at least one compliance obligation, satisfying the contextual linkage requirement auditors frequently examine.

**Clause 7: Support.** Clause 7.1 (Resources) documents resource allocation for the EMS including budget lines and personnel assignments. Clause 7.2 (Competence) links environmental roles to required competencies and training completion records. Clause 7.3 (Awareness) manages environmental awareness communications with confirmation records. Clause 7.4 (Communication) handles both internal and external environmental communications, including regulatory notifications and public disclosures, with a full communication log. Clause 7.5 (Documented information) applies the same controlled document framework used across all modules, with environmental procedure templates pre-loaded in the template library.

**Clause 8: Operation.** Clause 8.1 (Operational planning and control) captures operational controls for significant aspects, including elimination/substitution hierarchies and emergency preparedness linkage. Clause 8.2 (Emergency preparedness and response) connects to the Emergency Management module with scenario planning, response procedure documentation, exercise records, and post-incident review workflows. Clause 8.3 (Support of environmental direction of value chain) manages supplier environmental requirements and outsourced process controls.

**Clause 9: Performance Evaluation.** Clause 9.1 (Monitoring, measurement, analysis and evaluation) is supported by the environmental monitoring data module, where emission measurements, discharge readings, and consumption data are recorded against established baselines. Compliance evaluation records (9.1.2) capture periodic assessments of legal compliance with a structured rating scale and corrective action linkage. Clause 9.2 (Internal audit) draws on the shared audit management module with EMS-specific checklists. Clause 9.3 (Management review) aggregates environmental KPIs, significant aspects status, legal compliance summary, and objective progress into the management review agenda automatically.

**Clause 10: Improvement.** Clause 10.1 (General) and 10.2 (Nonconformity and corrective action) are served by the environmental CAPA module with root cause tools and effectiveness verification. Clause 10.3 (Continual improvement) is evidenced through the environmental performance trend dashboard, showing year-on-year improvement in key indicators such as energy intensity, waste diversion rate, water consumption, and GHG emissions per unit of output.

### 6.4 ISO 45001:2018 — Clause-by-Clause Coverage

ISO 45001:2018 replaced OHSAS 18001 as the international standard for occupational health and safety management systems. Its worker participation requirements, hazard identification methodology, and integration with legal compliance obligations represent more demanding requirements than its predecessor. Nexara's Health and Safety module was purpose-built to the ISO 45001 framework and is the platform's most feature-rich individual module.

**Clauses 1–3: Scope, Normative References, Terms and Definitions.** The OH&S Management System scope is configurable per legal entity and site, supporting complex multi-site organisations where the certification boundary varies by location or business unit. The term "worker" under ISO 45001 — encompassing employees, contractors, and visitors — is reflected in the platform's incident and risk recording, where worker type is a mandatory classification field.

**Clause 4: Context of the Organisation.** Clause 4.1 (Understanding the organisation and its context) captures internal and external issues with OH&S relevance: workforce demographics, operational hazard profiles, trade union relationships, regulatory environment, and industry sector risk profiles. Clause 4.2 (Understanding the needs and expectations of workers and other interested parties) is of particular importance under ISO 45001 given the standard's emphasis on worker participation. The platform's interested parties register distinguishes between workers and non-workers, with consultation and participation records linked to hazard identification activities (Clause 5.4). Clause 4.3 (Scope) records the OH&S scope with site-level granularity. Clause 4.4 (OH&S management system) links processes to hazard registers and legal obligations.

**Clause 5: Leadership and Worker Participation.** Clause 5.1 (Leadership and commitment) requires top management's active participation — evidenced through signed OH&S policy approvals, management review records, and resourcing decisions. Clause 5.2 (OH&S policy) manages version-controlled policy documents with full acknowledgement audit trails. Clause 5.3 (Organisational roles, responsibilities and authorities) links OH&S responsibilities to specific roles via the RBAC matrix. Clause 5.4 (Consultation and participation of workers) is uniquely addressed through the platform's worker consultation log, where hazard identification inputs, near-miss reports, toolbox talk records, and safety committee meeting minutes are captured and linked to the corresponding risk controls — providing the documented evidence of worker participation that ISO 45001 auditors specifically seek.

**Clause 6: Planning.** Clause 6.1.1 (Actions to address risks and opportunities for the OH&S MS) maintains a separate risk register for management system risks, distinct from operational hazard risks. Clause 6.1.2 (Hazard identification and assessment of risks and opportunities) is the platform's Risk Assessment module — the largest in the H&S system. Hazards are identified using configurable templates across eight hazard categories (physical, chemical, biological, ergonomic, psychosocial, electrical, mechanical, environmental). Each hazard is assigned a likelihood-consequence risk score (inherent) with linked control measures and a residual risk score. COSHH assessments, DSEAR assessments, and HAVS risk assessments are managed as specialist sub-types with tailored data fields. Clause 6.1.3 (Determination of legal and other requirements) maintains the OH&S Legal Register across UK health and safety legislation, including HASAWA 1974, COSHH, PUWER, LOLER, Manual Handling, CDM, DSE, and industry-specific regulations. Clause 6.1.4 (Planning action) generates action plans from hazard controls and legal compliance gaps. Clause 6.2 (OH&S objectives and planning) delivers SMART safety objectives with leading and lagging indicator tracking.

**Clause 7: Support.** Clause 7.1 (Resources) documents H&S budgets, equipment, and personnel allocations. Clause 7.2 (Competence) links roles to competency requirements and training records — mandatory certifications (IOSH, NEBOSH, first aiders, fire marshals) with expiry date tracking and automated renewal reminders. Clause 7.3 (Awareness) manages safety inductions, toolbox talks, and awareness campaigns with attendance records. Clause 7.4 (Communication) manages internal and external H&S communications, regulatory notifications (RIDDOR, Environment Agency), and enforcement body correspondence. Clause 7.5 (Documented information) applies the controlled document framework to all OH&S procedures, safe working systems, and risk assessment records.

**Clause 8: Operation.** Clause 8.1 (Operational planning and control) implements the hierarchy of controls — elimination, substitution, engineering controls, administrative controls, PPE — as a structured selection workflow within each risk assessment. Clause 8.1.3 (Management of change) operates the MOC workflow for new plant, processes, or activities. Clause 8.1.4 (Procurement) captures supplier and contractor health and safety pre-qualification requirements. Clause 8.2 (Emergency preparedness and response) connects to the Emergency Management module with site-specific emergency response plans, muster point records, evacuation drill completion, and business continuity linkage. The Permit to Work module (CDM Regulations compliance) manages hot work, confined space entry, electrical isolation, and working-at-height permits with digital authorisation workflows.

**Clause 9: Performance Evaluation.** Clause 9.1 (Monitoring, measurement, analysis and evaluation) tracks both leading indicators (near-miss reports submitted, toolbox talks delivered, risk assessments completed) and lagging indicators (RIDDOR reportable incidents, LTIs, AFR, LTIF, TRIR) with automated KPI computation. Clause 9.1.2 (Evaluation of compliance) manages periodic legal compliance assessments. Clause 9.2 (Internal audit) provides OH&S audit checklists mapped to each clause of ISO 45001. Clause 9.3 (Management review) automatically compiles OH&S performance data into the management review agenda.

**Clause 10: Improvement.** Clause 10.1 (General) and 10.2 (Incident, nonconformity and corrective action) deliver the Incident Management module — recording accidents, near-misses, dangerous occurrences, occupational diseases, and RIDDOR-reportable events with CAPA linkage, root cause analysis, and RIDDOR F2508 reporting support. Clause 10.3 (Continual improvement) is evidenced through safety performance trend dashboards and the near-miss to corrective action conversion rate — a recognised leading indicator of safety culture maturity.

### 6.5 ISO 27001:2022 — Controls and Annex A Coverage

ISO/IEC 27001:2022 is the premier international standard for information security management systems, significantly revised from its 2013 predecessor with 93 controls restructured across four control domains in Annex A. Nexara's Information Security module delivers comprehensive coverage of both the management system clauses (Clauses 4–10) and the full Annex A control framework, making it suitable as the technology backbone for organisations pursuing ISO 27001 certification.

**Management System Clauses (4–10).** The ISMS scope, context, interested parties, and information security policy are managed through the same structured framework applied to all Nexara management modules. Leadership commitment is evidenced through policy sign-off workflows, management review records, and audit programme ownership. Risk assessment (Clause 6.1.2) follows the ISO 27005 methodology with asset-based and scenario-based threat identification, likelihood-impact scoring, and risk treatment plans. The Statement of Applicability (SoA) — the critical output of Clause 6.1.3 — is generated directly from the platform's control register, showing applicability decisions, inclusion justifications, and implementation status for all 93 Annex A controls. Objectives (Clause 6.2), competence (Clause 7.2), and documented information (Clause 7.5) follow standard IMS workflows. Internal audit (Clause 9.2) provides ISO 27001-specific audit checklists. Management review (Clause 9.3) aggregates security metrics, incident summaries, risk treatment status, and supplier assessment outcomes.

**Annex A: Organisational Controls (5.1–5.37).** Nexara implements the complete set of 37 organisational controls. Policies for information security (5.1) are managed as version-controlled documents with review cycles. Information security roles and responsibilities (5.2) are embedded in the RBAC system with 39 defined roles across 17 modules. Threat intelligence (5.7) is served by the Regulatory Monitor module, which tracks CVE feeds and sector-specific threat advisories. Information security in project management (5.8) integrates security requirements into the Project Management module's project initiation phase. Information classification (5.12) and labelling (5.13) are implemented through document metadata tagging. Data leakage prevention (5.14) is addressed through API-level access controls, audit logging, and RBAC enforcement. Supplier relationships (5.19–5.22) are managed through the Supplier Management module with security questionnaire templates and audit right provisions. Information security incident management (5.24–5.28) delivers the full incident response lifecycle — from event detection through classification, response co-ordination, evidence preservation, and post-incident review. Business continuity for information security (5.29–5.30) integrates with the Emergency Management and Business Continuity modules. Legal, statutory, and contractual requirements (5.31–5.37) are addressed by the Legal Register, GDPR module, and Contract Management module, covering intellectual property, privacy protection, cryptography regulation, and records retention.

**Annex A: People Controls (6.1–6.8).** All eight people controls are implemented across the HR and Training modules. Screening (6.1) captures pre-employment check records and declarations. Terms and conditions of employment (6.2) links security responsibilities to employment records. Information security awareness, education, and training (6.3) is the Training module with mandatory IS awareness course assignments, completion tracking, and phishing simulation results. Disciplinary process (6.4) and responsibilities after termination (6.5) integrate with HR offboarding workflows, including system access revocation audit trails. Confidentiality agreements (6.6) are managed in the Contract module. Remote working (6.7) captures device registration and security posture assessments. Information security event reporting (6.8) provides the employee-facing incident reporting portal with anonymous near-miss capability.

**Annex A: Physical Controls (7.1–7.14).** Physical security perimeters (7.1), entry controls (7.2), physical security monitoring (7.4), and clear desk and screen policies (7.7) are documented through the physical security policy and control record modules. Equipment security (7.8–7.10), cabling security (7.12), and secure disposal (7.14) are tracked through the Asset module's physical asset records with maintenance and disposal audit trails.

**Annex A: Technological Controls (8.1–8.34).** This is the most extensive domain with 34 controls, all evidenced through Nexara's technical architecture and the InfoSec module. User endpoint devices (8.1) and privileged access rights (8.2) are governed by the RBAC system with session management and per-user rate limiting. Malware protection (8.7) and network filtering (8.20) are documented as organisational controls with technical evidence references. Web filtering (8.23), data masking (8.11), data leakage prevention (8.12), and information deletion (8.10) are implemented through API-level controls and the GDPR data management module. Logging (8.15), monitoring (8.16), and clock synchronisation (8.17) are delivered through the platform's Prometheus and Winston logging infrastructure with correlation IDs on every request. Configuration management (8.9) and technical vulnerability management (8.8) are supported by the patch management and CVE tracking features of the Regulatory Monitor module. Intrusion detection (8.16) integrates with the SIEM event correlation engine in the `@ims/security` package, applying six built-in detection rules across threshold, sequence, and velocity patterns. Secure development lifecycle (8.25–8.31) is evidenced through the SDLC documentation, code review records, and penetration testing reports managed in the InfoSec module. Cryptography (8.24) is implemented through the envelope encryption module in `@ims/security`, providing DEK/KEK key management with key rotation capability.

### 6.6 ISO 50001:2018 — Energy Management Coverage

ISO 50001:2018 establishes requirements for an energy management system (EnMS) and is particularly relevant to UK organisations subject to mandatory ESOS compliance and to organisations with material energy costs or Scope 1 and 2 GHG emissions reduction targets. Nexara's Energy Management module delivers clause-by-clause coverage through dedicated energy data models, an EnPI calculation engine, and structured energy review workflows.

**Clause 4: Context.** Organisational context issues with energy relevance — energy prices, grid decarbonisation trajectory, regulatory trajectory — are captured in the context register with energy-specific categorisation. Interested parties with energy-related requirements (energy regulators, ESOS assessors, investors, insurers) are maintained in the energy interested parties register.

**Clause 5: Leadership.** Top management commitment to the EnMS is evidenced through the energy policy approval workflow, the management review energy agenda item, and resource allocation approvals. The Energy Management Representative is designated through the RBAC system with explicit energy management role assignment.

**Clause 6: Planning.** The Energy Review (Clause 6.3) is the centrepiece of the ISO 50001 planning process. Nexara's Energy Review module guides practitioners through the structured review: identification of energy sources and uses, quantification of energy consumption by source and facility, identification of Significant Energy Uses (SEUs), and assessment of energy performance affecting variables. Past, present, and future energy consumption analysis is supported through time-series data visualisation with baseline adjustment for degree-days, production volume, and occupancy. Energy baselines (6.4) are established per reporting period with normalisation factor support. Energy Performance Indicators (EnPIs) (6.5) are configured as calculated KPIs drawing on metered consumption data, production data, and weather normalisation variables. The Energy Objectives, Targets, and Action Plans module (6.6) creates structured improvement projects with projected savings, actual savings tracking, and payback period calculation.

**Clause 7: Support.** Competence requirements for energy management roles — including ESOS Lead Assessor qualifications — are managed in the Competence Register with certificate expiry tracking. Document control covers energy procedures, measurement plans, and ESOS assessment reports. The metering plan document records the measurement and monitoring arrangements for all SEUs.

**Clause 8: Operation.** Operational controls for SEUs are documented as site-specific energy procedures linked to each SEU record. Design (Clause 8.2) captures energy considerations in capital project appraisals. Procurement (Clause 8.3) embeds energy efficiency criteria into supplier and equipment purchasing records.

**Clause 9: Performance Evaluation.** The monitoring and measurement framework (Clause 9.1) feeds live energy consumption data into the EnPI dashboard, with automated variance alerts when consumption deviates beyond the configurable threshold from the energy baseline. Internal audit (9.2) provides ISO 50001-specific audit checklists. Management review (9.3) aggregates energy performance, EnPI trend, SEU status, objective progress, and ESOS compliance status.

**Clause 10: Improvement.** Nonconformities identified during energy audits or EnPI variance analysis are processed through the standard CAPA workflow with root cause analysis and corrective action verification. The continual improvement dashboard shows cumulative energy savings achieved versus baseline, expressed in both absolute (MWh, MJ) and normalised (kWh per unit of output) terms, providing the evidence base for public sustainability reporting and SECR disclosures.

### 6.7 ISO 22301:2019 — Business Continuity Coverage

ISO 22301:2019 specifies requirements for a Business Continuity Management System (BCMS), providing the framework for organisations to protect against, reduce the likelihood of, prepare for, respond to, and recover from disruptive incidents. Nexara's Emergency Management and Business Continuity capability delivers comprehensive coverage of the standard's requirements, integrated with the Incident Management, Risk Management, and Crisis Communications modules.

**Clause 4: Context.** The BCMS scope is defined at the legal entity and site level, with the scope statement capturing which products, services, and activities are covered. Internal and external issues with business continuity relevance — supply chain concentration risk, single points of failure, critical infrastructure dependencies — are captured in the context register. Interested parties include insurers, regulators, major customers with contractual continuity requirements, and supply chain partners.

**Clause 5: Leadership.** Top management commitment to the BCMS is evidenced through business continuity policy approval, management review chairmanship records, and exercised authority over the Declaration of Incident and invocation of BCP. The Business Continuity Manager role is formally designated through the RBAC system.

**Clause 6: Planning.** Business Impact Analysis (BIA) is conducted within the platform's dedicated BIA module, where each critical business function is assessed against the standardised impact criteria — financial, reputational, operational, legal, and safety — across multiple time horizons (2 hours, 8 hours, 24 hours, 72 hours, 1 week). Maximum Tolerable Period of Disruption (MTPD) and Recovery Time Objectives (RTO) are captured per function and linked to corresponding recovery strategies. Recovery Point Objectives (RPO) for data-dependent processes are documented with evidence of backup frequency controls.

**Clause 7: Support.** The competence register tracks BCP team member training, tabletop exercise participation, and live exercise involvement. Business continuity awareness is managed through the Training module with annual awareness refresher assignments. The Document Control module maintains all BCPs, emergency response plans, and crisis communication scripts under version control.

**Clause 8: Operation.** Business Continuity Plans are created and maintained within the platform with structured templates covering: activation criteria and invocation authority, immediate response actions, resource requirements (people, technology, premises), recovery procedures, communication trees, and stand-down procedures. Crisis communication scripts and stakeholder notification lists are pre-configured and accessible during incident activation. The Exercise Programme module schedules and records tabletop exercises, functional exercises, and full simulation exercises, with post-exercise improvement actions tracked to closure. Supplier continuity requirements and alternative supplier arrangements are captured in the Supplier Management module with BCP-specific risk categorisation.

**Clause 9: Performance Evaluation.** Key BCMS metrics — exercise completion rate, BIA review currency, BCP version control currency, recovery time achieved versus RTO in exercises — are tracked on the Business Continuity dashboard. Internal audit provides ISO 22301-specific checklists covering all clauses. Management review includes BCMS performance, BIA change triggers, exercise outcomes, and incident experience.

**Clause 10: Improvement.** Post-incident reviews (PIR) following all activations — whether real or exercised — are processed through the CAPA module with lessons-learned records, BCP update requirements, and improvement action tracking. The continual improvement cycle is evidenced through the BCP change log, showing how exercise findings and real-incident experience have progressively strengthened the organisation's recovery capability.

### 7.1 Market Landscape Overview

**Market Size and Composition**

The addressable market for enterprise management system software occupies the intersection of three historically distinct but rapidly converging software categories. The Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) market, valued at $15.3 billion in 2025 and growing at 13.8% CAGR, encompasses risk registers, audit management, regulatory compliance tracking, and policy management. The Environmental, Health, and Safety (EHS) market, valued at $8.4 billion in 2025 and growing at 14.7% CAGR, covers hazard identification, incident management, environmental monitoring, permit management, and occupational hygiene. The Quality Management System (QMS) market, valued at $7.2 billion in 2025 and growing at 12.9% CAGR, addresses non-conformance management, document control, calibration, supplier quality, and audit management. The combined total addressable market exceeds $31 billion and is expanding at a compound annual growth rate of 14.2% as regulatory complexity intensifies, ESG reporting becomes mandatory, and organisations seek to consolidate fragmented software estates.

**Market Trends Reshaping the Competitive Landscape**

Four structural forces are reshaping buying behaviour across this market and creating the conditions for a platform disruptor to emerge.

First, platform consolidation is accelerating. CIOs and CFOs in regulated industries are under increasing pressure to reduce software vendor count, eliminate data silos, and lower the total cost of compliance. Analyst research consistently shows that organisations managing quality, EHS, and GRC through separate vendor relationships spend 40–60% more on compliance operations than those on unified platforms, factoring in integration maintenance, duplicate data entry, cross-system reconciliation, and the employee burden of navigating multiple user interfaces.

Second, the AI integration mandate is reshaping vendor expectations. Buyers now evaluate management system platforms not merely on their module breadth but on the quality and depth of their AI-assisted workflows. Capabilities such as automated root cause analysis suggestions, predictive non-conformance detection, natural language regulatory change summaries, and AI-assisted audit finding classification have moved from differentiating to expected features in enterprise-tier evaluations. Nexara's native `@ims/nlq` natural language query engine, integrated AI Analysis module, and SIEM-grade behavioural analytics represent a genuine capability advantage over incumbents whose AI features are superficial overlays on ageing architectures.

Third, mandatory ESG and climate reporting requirements are creating an entirely new software buying motion. With the UK's Companies Act ESG disclosure requirements, SEC climate disclosure rules for UK-listed companies, CSRD mandatory reporting for EU-operating organisations, and TCFD becoming embedded in UK corporate governance, finance and sustainability teams are acquiring EHS and environmental management platforms that previously were the exclusive domain of safety and quality functions. This cross-functional buying motion favours integrated platforms with finance-grade reporting outputs over specialist EHS tools.

Fourth, the ISO certification market continues to expand. UKAS accredited ISO 9001 certificates in the UK grew 8.2% in 2024. ISO 14001 certificates grew 11.4%. ISO 45001 certificates grew 19.3% as organisations migrated from the now-withdrawn OHSAS 18001. ISO 27001 certificates grew 22.6% as supply chain cybersecurity requirements intensified. Each new certification creates a corresponding management system software requirement, expanding the total addressable opportunity.

### 7.2 Competitor Deep-Dive: Ideagen

Ideagen plc (LSE: IDEA, acquired by Hg Capital in 2022) is the dominant UK-headquartered compliance software vendor, formed through the acquisition of more than fifteen legacy GRC and quality management platforms including Pentana Solutions, Huddle (document collaboration), Teammate (internal audit), Coruson (QHSE), and Gael (aerospace quality). Its breadth of acquisition gives it recognisable brand names across multiple buying centres, which represents its primary commercial advantage.

**Strengths.** Ideagen's depth in regulated sectors — aerospace (Gael), nuclear (Qualtrax integration), and financial services audit (Teammate) — gives it credibility with compliance functions in those verticals. Its established UKAS and BSI partnership relationships provide warm pathways to certification body-sponsored sales. The Huddle document collaboration product provides genuine, deeply integrated document management that some competitors lack.

**Weaknesses.** The fundamental challenge for Ideagen is architectural: its portfolio comprises fifteen or more separately engineered platforms, each with its own data model, authentication system, and UI paradigm. Integration between Coruson (QHSE) and Teammate (audit) requires bespoke professional services engagement. There is no single data model across the estate — meaning a non-conformance in Coruson is invisible in Teammate without manual export. Pricing reflects this complexity: commercial engagements typically range from £500/month for small Coruson or Teammate standalone deployments to £5,000–£15,000/month for multi-module enterprise configurations, with significant additional professional services costs for cross-product integration. ESG reporting, AI management (ISO 42001), anti-bribery (ISO 37001), and energy management (ISO 50001) have no native coverage. The absence of a modern API-first architecture limits integration with ERP, HR, and finance systems. Nexara's unified data model, modern REST API with 2,558 documented endpoints, and native coverage of 22 standards present a compelling architectural contrast in head-to-head evaluations.

# Facilitator Master Notes — Nexara IMS Administrator Training

**For facilitators only. Do not distribute to participants.**

---

## Your Role as Facilitator

You are not delivering a lecture. You are guiding experienced professionals through a structured skills-building experience. Participants arrive with domain knowledge; your job is to accelerate their transition from user to administrator.

**Three principles to keep front of mind:**
1. **Credibility first** — Know the platform deeply. If you don't know something, say so and commit to following up.
2. **Pace over coverage** — It is better to cover 80% well than 100% superficially. Flag skipped content in the participant handbook.
3. **Psychological safety** — Administrators will make mistakes in production. Create a space where it is safe to ask "stupid" questions.

---

## Tone & Language

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Use precise technical language: "SCIM endpoint", "permission namespace" | Dumb it down: "the connector thingy" |
| Say "this is a common mistake" to normalise errors | Say "obviously" or "as you know" |
| Use "we" — you're solving problems together | Position yourself as the expert talking at them |
| Acknowledge if a participant knows more than you about their industry | Pretend you know their business better than they do |
| Say "good question" only when it actually is | Overuse praise — it becomes meaningless |

## Pacing Guidance

### Day 1 Rhythm

**Morning (08:30–12:15)**
- Energy is highest. Use this for conceptual density — RBAC, SCIM, permission inheritance.
- Pre-assessment (09:00): Allow 15 minutes for the 20 questions. Then use results diagnostically during breaks.
- Module 1 (09:00–10:30): Start with a live demo before theory. Show user creation, then explain the SCIM spec.
- Module 2 (10:45–12:15): Role matrix exercise is the centrepiece. Allow 25 minutes for the group exercise.

**Post-lunch dip (13:00–14:00)**
- Energy is lowest, typically 30–60 minutes after lunch.
- Module 3 starts here. Use a hands-on lab activity in the first 20 minutes to re-engage.
- Avoid dense slide content between 13:15 and 13:45.

**Afternoon recovery (14:00–17:00)**
- Energy gradually recovers. Module 4 (integrations) is procedural — good for this slot.
- Day 1 formative (16:15): Keep to 15 minutes for questions; 15 minutes for whole-group review.
- End strong: preview Day 2 as "the operational side — where you'll feel most like an administrator."

### Day 2 Rhythm

**Morning (08:30–12:15)**
- Day 1 recap should be fast — 15 minutes max. Participants know this material.
- Module 5 (Audit): This is often the most engaging module. Lean into the incident investigation scenario.
- Module 6 (Backup/Restore): Procedural and hands-on. Keep it practical.

**Post-lunch (13:00–14:00)**
- Module 7 is shorter (60 min) — use this compressed slot intentionally.
- Leave the last 10 minutes for participants to ask any lingering questions before assessment.

**Assessment block (14:15–15:45)**
- Set the room: silence, individual work, no collaboration.
- For virtual delivery: use breakout rooms (one person per room) or honour system.
- Mark Part B scenarios as a facilitator between 15:15 and 15:30.
- Debrief: Go through each MCQ question that more than 2 participants got wrong.

## Inclusive Facilitation

### Neurodiversity

- Provide written instructions for all activities alongside verbal instructions.
- Give advance notice of group exercises ("In 10 minutes we'll do a group activity...").
- Allow quiet solo work time before group discussion — not everyone thinks out loud.
- Print materials are preferable to screen-only; offer both.

### Language & Cultural Diversity

- Avoid idioms that don't translate: "nail it", "ballpark", "low-hanging fruit".
- Allow non-native English speakers extra time in exercises (build this into timings).
- Write key technical terms on the whiteboard — visual reference reduces cognitive load.
- For virtual delivery: use chat as a parallel input channel for quieter participants.

### Technical Confidence Gaps

- Some participants will feel intimidated by the technical depth. Watch for disengagement.
- Pair experienced and less experienced participants in lab exercises.
- Frame labs as "let's figure this out together" not "complete this task".

### Experience Gaps

- Some participants may have years of sysadmin experience; others may be business users stepping into a new role.
- Use the pre-assessment results (available to facilitator only) to identify gaps and direct attention.
- Encourage experienced participants to mentor during labs — this reinforces their learning too.

## Energy Management Techniques

| Situation | Technique |
|-----------|-----------|
| Post-lunch energy dip | Stand-up activity: "name one thing you'd configure differently after this morning" |
| Over-running on content | "I'm going to flag the next section as self-study in the handbook and move on" |
| Participant dominating discussion | "Great point — let's hear from someone who hasn't spoken yet" |
| Participant disengaged on phone | Break activity that requires their input specifically |
| Technical question you can't answer | "I'll get you a definitive answer in writing by end of day — what's your email?" |
| Group exercise taking too long | Give a 2-minute warning, then do a "share one insight" round-robin |
| Assessment anxiety | "This assessment is about your learning journey, not a test of your worth as a professional" |

## Lab Facilitation

- **Observe, don't solve.** When a participant is stuck, ask "what would you check first?" not "here's what to do."
- **Time-box generously.** Add 10% to all lab time estimates for first delivery.
- **Capture blockers.** Note what labs consistently trip participants — this is feedback for content improvement.
- **Extension activities.** Fast finishers should attempt the "live extension" in each lab — never give them idle time.
- **Shared screens.** For virtual delivery, ask a volunteer to share their screen during labs so the group can see the process.

## Handling Common Difficult Situations

### "Our environment is different from this"
**Response**: "Absolutely — every deployment has its specific configuration. The principles we're covering are universal; the specifics you'll adapt. Make a note in your handbook of anything that needs translating to your context."

### "Why doesn't the platform do X?"
**Response**: "That's a product question rather than a training question — let me take that as a feature request to our product team. For now, let's focus on what's available today."

### "I already knew this"
**Response**: "Great — your job during this section is to be the expert peer for the group. If you notice anything I've missed or a nuance from your experience, please add it."

### Technical failure during demo
**Response**: "Perfect timing — this is exactly what we'll cover in Module 6. Let me show you how to handle this. [Continue from local backup or slide screenshots.]"

### Participant significantly failing labs
**Response**: Private check-in during break. "I noticed the lab was challenging — what specifically felt unclear? Let's work through it before the next module."

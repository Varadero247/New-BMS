| Q | Correct Answer | Option | Explanation |
|---|---------------|--------|-------------|
| 1 | B | The programme provides a managed view of all planned audits, supports risk-based scheduling, and enables programme-level reporting | ISO 9001:2015 clause 9.2.2 requires organisations to establish and maintain audit programmes — not just individual audits. A programme record enables: (a) annual programme-level reporting (audits planned vs completed, finding rate trends), (b) risk-based scheduling driven by NC data, and (c) a single view that can be presented to the certification body as evidence of a managed audit function. Option C is incorrect — standalone audit records can be created in Nexara; the programme structure is best practice, not a system constraint. |
| 2 | B | Minor NC — a single, isolated instance of non-fulfilment with no systemic impact | One missing signature out of 47 records reviewed is an isolated, low-impact instance. The process is generally functioning; this is not a systemic failure. Minor NC is the correct classification for a single instance of non-fulfilment without systemic impact. A Major NC would require evidence of systemic failure (e.g., 30 of 47 records missing signatures, or no signing process in place). An OFI is a positive recommendation, not appropriate here. An Observation is used when a risk exists but no actual non-conformance has occurred — a missing signature is a confirmed non-fulfilment. |
| 3 | C | OFI — this is a positive practice that could be beneficially applied in other departments | An OFI (Opportunity for Improvement) is a positive, proactive recommendation. The QR code indexing practice exceeds the standard's requirements and demonstrates innovative good practice. The auditor's recommendation that this practice could be adopted more broadly is an OFI. Conformance (A) is technically correct — the clause requirement is met — but it does not capture the positive practice. Option D is incorrect: using a practice not specified in the procedure does not constitute an NC if the clause requirement is met. |
| 4 | B | A systemic failure to meet a standard requirement, or a failure posing significant risk to product safety, customer satisfaction, or management system integrity | Major NC = systemic (repeated, widespread, or fundamental) failure, or a single failure with significant impact. The key distinguishing factor from Minor NC is systemic nature or significant risk. A single documentation error (A) is typically Minor. Clause 8 findings are not automatically Major (C). Inability to provide records at the opening meeting (D) would be an Observation at most — the opening meeting is an introduction, not the evidence collection phase. |
| 5 | B | The annual programme record, individual audit records, finding records with ISO clause references, and the distributed audit report | ISO 9001:2015 clause 9.2.2(f) requires retaining documented information as evidence of the audit programme implementation and audit results. "Audit programme" requires the programme record; "audit results" requires finding records (what was found) and audit reports (what was concluded and distributed). The audit schedule alone (A) is insufficient — it shows what was planned, not what was found. Management review minutes (D) summarise findings but are not the primary evidence. |
| 6 | C | Stopping before five levels risks identifying a symptom rather than the root cause, leading to corrective actions that produce temporary improvement followed by recurrence | The 5-Why's value is in drilling through successive causal layers to the fundamental cause. Most practitioners who "stop early" stop at Why 2 or Why 3 — at symptoms (e.g., "human error") rather than root causes (e.g., "the procedure does not exist" or "the system configuration permitted the error"). Corrective actions targeting symptoms produce temporary results. The five-level enforcement is a quality gate, not an ISO clause requirement (A is incorrect). |
| 7 | C | Fishbone (Ishikawa) — multiple causes across Man, Machine, Method, Material, and Measurement categories are present simultaneously | The scenario describes five distinct contributing causes from five different categories: Man (fatigue), Machine (guard removed), Method (permit PPE gap), Material (PPE not available), Measurement (no pre-task PPE check). This multi-category, simultaneous-cause scenario is the textbook use case for fishbone analysis. 5-Why (A) would produce a single linear chain that misses the multi-dimensional nature of the event. Fault tree (B) is used for very complex, safety-critical events where AND/OR gate logic is necessary — appropriate for aerospace/nuclear, less so for a straightforward PPE incident. Option D is wrong — the choice of technique materially affects the completeness of the root cause identification and the resulting corrective actions. |
| 8 | B | The NC or finding has recurred; the original CAPA is closed as Ineffective and a new CAPA must be raised with a deeper or different root cause analysis method | Ineffective is a terminal status — it means the corrective action failed to prevent recurrence. The correct response is not to extend monitoring (A) or re-submit evidence (D) — the CAPA has been evaluated and found ineffective. A new CAPA is required, and it must use a different or deeper RCA approach, because the first root cause analysis evidently did not identify the actual root cause. Partially Effective (not Ineffective) would be the status if some actions worked and some did not (A is describing partial effectiveness, not Ineffective). |
| 9 | C | Escalate to senior management as a management review input; three or more repeat findings against the same clause indicate a systemic CAPA failure requiring strategic intervention | Three or more repeat findings against the same clause is an explicit escalation trigger in Nexara. This pattern indicates that the corrective action process is not resolving the underlying cause — a conclusion that requires senior management attention and strategic resource allocation, not just another CAPA. A 26% repeat findings rate is already above the 20% programme review threshold. Contacting the certification body (D) is not the appropriate internal response. |
| 10 | B | CAPAs can originate from multiple sources and are all visible in the All CAPAs view, filterable by source type | This is a key Day E learning point: CAPA is a cross-module function in Nexara. The All CAPAs view provides the organisation-wide corrective action picture that senior management and auditors need. Options A, C, and D are all incorrect: reference formats are the same across source types; any user with CAPA create permission can create them; management review output actions and CAPA actions are distinct record types on separate dashboards. |
| 11 | B | The status of actions from previous management reviews | ISO 9001:2015 clause 9.3.2(a) explicitly lists "the status of actions from previous management reviews" as a required input. This ensures that output actions are tracked to completion and that the review is iterative rather than isolated. Options A (strategic business plan), C (quality costs financial summary), and D (external benchmarking) are not required inputs under clause 9.3.2, though they may be useful additions. |
| 12 | B | It compiles performance data from all selected modules into the review inputs in real time, with timestamps and source links | The input compiler is the mechanism for automatically aggregating cross-module performance data into the management review inputs — eliminating the manual data gathering process. It produces timestamped, source-linked data that is traceable to the underlying records. It does not auto-generate the agenda (A), draft output actions (C), or manage attendee invitations (D). |
| 13 | C | "Commission an external gap assessment against ISO 37001:2016 to assess anti-bribery management system readiness — owner: Legal Director — due: 90 days" | Management review output actions should be strategic decisions made by top management — resource allocation, strategic direction, commissioning of new initiatives. Options A, B, and D are operational corrective actions that belong in the CAPA module: they address specific process failures at an operational level. Option C is a strategic decision by top management to assess and potentially expand the organisation's management system scope — this is precisely the kind of output expected from a management review under ISO 9001 clause 9.3.3. |
| 14 | B | The input compiler has run and data has been populated; the review meeting should now be held, agenda recorded, minutes captured, and output actions created | "Inputs Compiled" means the data is ready for the meeting — it is a preparation state, not a completion state. The review meeting must still occur; minutes and output actions must be recorded; the chairperson must approve the minutes. Only then does the status progress to Complete. Option A conflates input compilation with completion. Option C is incorrect — inputs are compiled before the meeting, not during it. |
| 15 | B | The `nextReviewDate` triggers an overdue notification if a new review has not been created in Planned status by that date | Nexara enforces planned intervals by monitoring the `nextReviewDate` and notifying relevant users when it passes without a new review record being created. This creates an audit trail of the intended interval and the actual timing of the review. Option A is incorrect — the system does not lock the module. Option C is incorrect — review intervals are set by the QMS team on each review record. Option D is incorrect — the system does continue to notify after the initial reminder if no action is taken. |
| 16 | A | The completed management review record, plus the overdue notification logged in the audit trail, plus the corrective action taken to prevent recurrence | This three-part combination addresses the examiner's concern directly: (1) the completed record proves the review was eventually held; (2) the audit trail overdue notification proves the planned interval was understood (it was "planned" — a date was set — and the system recorded when it was missed); (3) the corrective action demonstrates the organisation has identified the root cause of the delay and taken steps to prevent recurrence. An email explanation (B) alone is insufficient — it is not a documented system record. The output action register (C) covers previous review outputs, not interval adherence. |
| 17 | B | The compiled inputs report, generated by the input compiler — it records all data sources consulted, the data extracted, and the timestamp of extraction, mapped to the clause 9.3.2 input list | The compiled inputs report is the evidence that all required clause 9.3.2 inputs were actually considered, not just that the review occurred. It records the specific data, when it was extracted, and from which module — providing a complete, traceable record that the certification body can verify. Manual checklist (D) is an acceptable alternative in a manual system but is not the Nexara approach. |
| 18 | B | The output actions from the previous management review are all assigned to the QMS Manager alone, with no actions assigned to the Managing Director, CFO, or other senior leaders | ISO 9001 clause 9.3 requires review by "top management." If all output actions are assigned to the QMS Manager, this suggests that top management attended and noted the review but made no personal commitments — the review had no strategic impact on senior decision-making. This is a classic sign of a management review that is compliance-driven rather than genuine engagement. Option A (3-week delay with explanation) is explainable. Option C (inputs extracted the day before) is good practice — timely data. Option D (Minor NC reference in minutes) is expected content, not a concern. |
| 19 | B | The audit finding records (with ISO clause references and classification), the linked CAPA records (with RCA completed), the corrective action status, and the effectiveness review outcomes | This combination provides the complete audit-to-CAPA-to-effectiveness chain: what was found (finding records with clause references), how it was investigated (RCA in CAPA), what was done (corrective action status), and whether it worked (effectiveness review). The KPI dashboard (A) shows aggregate metrics but not individual finding management. Management review minutes (C) summarise findings but are not the primary evidence chain. Distribution log (D) is evidence that the report was shared, not that findings were managed. |
| 20 | B | The NC record + the linked CAPA record (including root cause analysis, action plan, and effectiveness review outcome with uploaded evidence of non-recurrence) | ISO 9001:2015 clause 10.2.2 requires two categories of evidence: (a) the nature of the NCs and actions taken — addressed by the NC record (description, category, containment) and the CAPA action plan; and (b) the results of corrective action — addressed by the effectiveness review outcome and uploaded non-recurrence evidence. The NC record alone (A) does not contain the CAPA effectiveness evidence. The CAPA record alone (C) does not contain the full nature of the NC. Management review minutes (D) are a summary reference, not the primary documented evidence. |

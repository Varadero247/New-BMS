## 09:00 — Content Block 1: Incidents

**Opening**: "Let's start with the most operationally urgent part of HSE management — what happens in the first 60 minutes after an incident is reported. The system is designed to make the most stressful moment of your week as structured as possible."

**Key facilitation point — severity classification**: When covering MINOR to CATASTROPHIC, use real examples from the news (without identifying specific organisations). The classification table in the module content gives clear criteria. Common participant error: classifying hospitalisation as MODERATE. Clarify: "Hospitalisation typically indicates MAJOR unless the person was kept in for observation only (less than 4 hours) and returned to full duties the next day."

**Key facilitation point — TRIR formula**: Many participants have calculated TRIR manually and will have variations in their methodology. Validate their method, then show how Nexara automates this. "The system uses 200,000 as the multiplier — the industry standard based on 100 workers × 50 weeks × 40 hours. Your safety statistics reporting to your regulator should use the same denominator."

---

## 10:45 — Content Block 2: Legal Register & Environmental

**Opening**: "The legal register is the documentary backbone of your HSE management system. Without it, you cannot demonstrate to an auditor that you know what your obligations are, let alone that you're meeting them."

**Key teaching point — compliance status**: "AT_RISK is not an admission of non-compliance — it's the professional acknowledgement that you're aware of a gap and actively working to close it. Auditors prefer AT_RISK with an action plan to COMPLIANT with no evidence. Never mark something COMPLIANT unless you have evidence."

**Significance scoring exercise**: After explaining the formula, give participants 5 minutes to calculate a score for "Chemical storage and handling" using the following values: severity=4, probability=3, duration=2, extent=2, reversibility=3, regulatory=5, stakeholder=4. Expected score: (4×1.5)+(3×1.5)+2+2+3+5+4 = 6+4.5+16 = **26.5** → Significant. Walk through the answer.

---

## 13:00 — Content Block 3: PTW & Inspections

**Opening**: "If legal register is the documentary backbone, PTW is the operational nerve system — the mechanism by which you authorise high-risk work before it starts. The key word is before. The scenario in today's lab is based on what happens when a PTW is not in place."

**Common Q**: "Can contractors submit their own PTW request?" Answer: "Contractors with Nexara portal access can submit a PTW request, but the approval chain always includes an internal approver. The permit is only valid once the internal approver chain is complete."

**Inspection classification Q**: "Is a wet floor finding an Observation or a Minor NC?" Answer: "It depends on whether a procedure exists. If a housekeeping procedure requires dry floors and the floor is wet, it's a Minor NC (procedure not followed). If no procedure exists, it's an Observation and the absence of a procedure is a System NC."

## Q&A Management

| Question | Answer |
|---------|--------|
| "Do we need to notify Nexara Support if a CRITICAL incident is recorded?" | "No — Nexara IMS is a management system, not a regulator. You notify your regulatory authority directly. The system's regulatory reportable flag generates a draft notification for your records." |
| "Can we set up automatic RIDDOR notifications?" | "The system generates a draft notification. Actual submission to the regulator must be done by a named authorised person — this is an intentional design choice to prevent automated submission of regulatory reports." |
| "What if the PTW approver chain includes someone who is on annual leave?" | "The PTW applicant can request a delegated approver via Actions → Reassign Approval. This is logged in the permit audit trail." |

# Day 1 Facilitation Guide — Foundations

**Programme**: Nexara IMS Train-the-Trainer (T3)
**Audience**: Nexara Master Trainer (this document is not distributed to participants)
**Purpose**: Full lead-trainer script for Day 1 with timing, Q&A prompts, and transition language

---

## Pre-Session Setup (07:30–08:30)

Arrive 60 minutes before start. Complete the following in order:

1. **Room**: Confirm horseshoe or cabaret configuration; adjust if needed; ensure all participants can see the display and each other
2. **Technology**: Log in to sandbox (`admin@ims.local` / `admin123`); confirm all demo data is present; load the Training Portal in a second browser tab
3. **Materials**: Distribute participant packs; place peer feedback forms face-down at seats; post the Parking Lot flipchart sheet at the side of the room
4. **Name cards**: Set out pre-printed name cards (tented A5 card); check spelling against attendance list
5. **Trainer desk**: Open `DAY-1-FACILITATION-GUIDE.md` and `MODULE-ADULT-LEARNING.md` on your device; open the segment list for the micro-teaching lab (to be completed during lunch)

## 08:30 — Welcome (30 min)

### Opening (3 min)

*Stand at the front, not behind a desk. Make eye contact with each participant as they settle.*

**Script**:
> "Good morning, everyone. Welcome to Day 1 of the Nexara Train-the-Trainer programme. My name is [Name] and I'm a Nexara Master Trainer — which means I've been certified by Nexara Learning & Development to deliver this specific programme. You're in the right room.
>
> By end of tomorrow, some of you — those who pass both assessments — will be authorised to deliver Nexara training independently to your colleagues. That's what this is about. Not sitting through content, but becoming the person who delivers it.
>
> So let's start."

---

### Introductions (15 min)

**Prompt** (address the group):
> "I'd like to hear from each of you. Tell us your name, your role, and — this is important — just one word that describes your current confidence about standing up and training people. One word. Doesn't have to be positive."

*Listen actively; repeat each person's name; note confidence words on a small flipchart or whiteboard. These become data — you'll reference them at the Day 2 close.*

**Trainer note**: If someone says "terrified" — acknowledge it positively: "Perfect. Terrified people pay attention. Let's use that."

---

### Programme Overview (7 min)

**Script**:
> "Here's what the two days look like. [Display agenda.] Today is foundations — understanding how adults learn, how to facilitate, and how to navigate the Nexara curriculum you'll be teaching. Tomorrow is certification — you'll assess participants, manage a programme administratively, and at 1pm you'll each deliver a 20-minute segment while I observe and score you.
>
> Two assessments. Written — 20 questions, 30 minutes, 75% to pass. Delivery — 20 minutes, five competency domains, 70% to pass. Both required for certification. If you pass the written and not the delivery, you get a resubmission opportunity within 30 days. If you pass neither, you come back to the next cohort. Questions on that structure now?"

*Pause. Wait genuinely. Do not fill the silence immediately.*

---

### Psychological Contract (5 min)

**Script**:
> "Here's what I commit to you: I'll be honest, I'll give you feedback you can use, and I'll not waste your time with content you don't need. Here's what I need from you: engage — ask questions, challenge things that don't make sense, and when it's your turn to practise, practise seriously.
>
> One more thing. The people who don't pass are usually the ones who weren't fully present. Not because they lacked knowledge — because they were checking emails during the facilitation skills module. So: devices on silent, notifications off, and you'll be fine."

**Transition**:
> "Alright. Let's talk about why adults learn the way they do — because if you don't understand this, everything else in the programme falls apart."

## 09:00 — Module 1: Adult Learning Theory (90 min)

*Refer to `MODULE-ADULT-LEARNING.md` for full content. Use the following script for section openings and transitions.*

### 09:00 — Andragogy (20 min)

**Opening**:
> "Knowles identified four things that make adult learning different from child learning. Not better or worse — just different. And all four of them matter when you're trying to teach someone to use a compliance system they may not be excited about using.
>
> The four principles are: [display slide or list]. Read them. Now tell your neighbour which one you think is most violated in typical compliance training. Go — 90 seconds."

*Allow the pair discussion; bring back two or three responses.*

**Common responses to validate**:
- "Relevance — they don't see why it matters to their job" → "Exactly. That's the one you'll fix by contextualising every Nexara feature to their actual workflow."
- "Experience — trainers don't build on what they already know" → "Yes. Start with what participants already do manually, then show how Nexara automates it."

---

### 09:20 — Bloom's Taxonomy (20 min)

**Opening**:
> "Bloom's tells us what level of thinking we're targeting. 'Know that X exists' is not the same as 'be able to do X when it matters.' For compliance training, we always want to land at Apply. Memorising where the NC form is doesn't help anyone. Actually raising an NC, correctly, under pressure, does."

**Q&A prompt** (at 09:35):
> "At what Bloom's level does the End User Training assessment operate? You've seen the questions. Think about it — open, closed, knowledge or application?"

*Expected: Application — they must apply platform knowledge to scenarios, not just recall button names.*

---

### 09:40 — Learning Styles (15 min)

**Script**:
> "VARK. Visual, Auditory, Reading/Writing, Kinaesthetic. The research on VARK is more contested than it used to be — some researchers question whether fixed learning styles exist at all. What IS reliable: when you use multiple modalities in one session, more people retain more content. So: show it (visual), explain it (auditory), reference it in materials (R/W), and make them do it (kinaesthetic). Every module in the Nexara curriculum covers all four."

**Common mistake warning**:
> "Watch out for this: 'I'm a visual learner' sometimes means 'I prefer to be passive and watch.' That's not the same thing. Kinaesthetic — doing — has the highest retention rates. Make your participants do things in the sandbox, not just watch you do them."

---

### 09:55 — Cognitive Load (15 min)

**Script**:
> "There are three types of cognitive load: intrinsic (the difficulty of the content itself), extraneous (complexity you add unnecessarily — cluttered slides, jargon, tangents), and germane (the mental work of actually learning). Your job as a trainer is to minimise extraneous load so participants can invest cognitive effort in germane load.
>
> Practical implication for Nexara training: don't try to teach seven modules in one session. Don't use database column names (use field labels). Don't show a blank form before showing a completed example."

---

### 10:10 — Compliance Training Psychology (15 min)

**Script**:
> "Here's the hard truth: most compliance training is designed to be survived, not learned. It's a box to tick. Your participants may show up expecting that. Your job is to disrupt that expectation in the first five minutes.
>
> How? Relevance and story. 'Let me tell you about an incident that happened at a company like yours because someone didn't know how to use this module.' That's not manipulation — that's demonstrating why it matters."

**Discussion prompt**:
> "What's one technique — from what we've discussed in the last 90 minutes — that you could apply in the first five minutes of an End User session to break the box-ticking expectation? You have two minutes to write it down, then we'll hear three examples."

---

### 10:25 — Module 1 Close (5 min)

**Script**:
> "Before the break — one reflection question. Write this down: Which of the five areas we covered will most change how you prepare a training session? Adult learning principles, Bloom's, VARK, cognitive load, or compliance psychology? There's no right answer. Keep it — we'll come back to it tomorrow."

**Parking Lot check**: Any questions written? Address any quick ones now; defer complex to the debrief.

**Transition to break**:
> "Take 15 minutes. Be back at 10:45 for the most practice-intensive part of today."

---

## 10:45 — Module 2: Facilitation Skills (90 min)

*Refer to `MODULE-FACILITATION-SKILLS.md` for full content.*

### Opening (2 min)

> "Module 1 was conceptual. Module 2 is practical. By the end of this 90 minutes, you should be able to name and use four questioning techniques, handle a difficult participant without the room noticing it's difficult, and recognise when a room's energy is dropping before it crashes."

### Questioning Demonstration (11:00–11:25)

**Script before demonstration**:
> "I'm going to ask four questions. After each one, tell me: what type of question was it, and what was it designed to do?"

*Direct questions at specific participants by name:*
- **Open**: "[Name], what do you find most challenging about explaining technical content to non-technical staff?" (Elicits personal experience; no wrong answer)
- **Probing**: "Interesting — can you say more about why that's challenging? What specifically makes it hard?" (Deepens the response)
- **Hypothetical**: "[Name], if you had a participant who completely refused to engage with the sandbox — how would you respond?" (Applies thinking to a scenario)
- **Rhetorical**: "Because if you don't know what question to ask next, how can you know if learning is happening?" (No answer expected; makes a point)

*After four questions:*
> "Those four types form the core of facilitation. Open to start discussion. Probing to deepen it. Hypothetical to apply it. Rhetorical to emphasise a point. You will use all four tomorrow."

### Role-Play: Difficult Participants (11:25–11:45)

**Setup**:
> "I'm going to describe four participant archetypes that you will definitely encounter. After each description, I want your suggestion for how to handle them — then I'll give you the Nexara-approved approach."

*Use `MODULE-FACILITATION-SKILLS.md` Section 3 for the four archetypes and handling strategies.*

**Energy note**: This is intentionally engaging. Allow some humour — trainers recognise these archetypes from their own experience. Do not let it become a complaint session about past participants.

## 14:30 — Lab: Micro-Teaching (75 min)

*See `LAB-DAY-1.md` for full lab instructions.*

**Trainer script before first delivery**:
> "Here's how this works. Each of you delivers for 10 minutes. Two of your peers observe using the feedback form. I observe everyone but don't interrupt. After each delivery I'll give the group one sentence of observation — individual feedback is in the debrief.
>
> To the first delivery: this is practice, not assessment. Nobody is being graded today. The goal is honest effort and honest feedback. [Name], you're first — over to you."

*Step back. Sit at the side. Take notes quietly. Do not react visibly (positively or negatively) during delivery.*

---

## 15:45 — Debrief (45 min)

**Opening**:
> "Brilliant work from everyone. Before I share patterns I observed, I want to know what you saw. What did the strongest deliveries have in common?"

*Facilitate a 10-minute open discussion. Write responses on the flipchart.*

**Trainer observations** (10 min):
- Share two or three patterns observed across all deliveries — not individual feedback by name
- Example patterns: "Most of you started with a fact, not a question — consider which works better for your audience"; "Three of you apologised for the content — don't; own it"

**Individual peer feedback** (5 min): Return the written peer feedback forms to each participant.

**Curriculum Q&A** (15 min): Open floor.

**Parking Lot review** (5 min): Address any outstanding items.

---

## 16:30 — Formative Check + Day 2 Briefing (30 min)

**Formative check script**:
> "This is for you, not for me. 10 questions. Work individually, then check with your neighbour. The answers are on the back of the form. This tells you where to focus tonight."

**Day 2 briefing script**:
> "Tomorrow: your assessed delivery is at [times — read from schedule]. Written assessment is at 15:15. Don't prepare a lecture — prepare a facilitated segment. The observer checklist rewards interaction, not presentation.
>
> Tonight: one final run-through of your assigned segment, in front of a mirror or to your camera. Time yourself. 20 minutes. Not 18, not 22 — 20."

**Close**:
> "One last thing. Look at the confidence words you gave this morning. [Read a selection aloud.] Tomorrow, I expect to see something different. See you at 08:30."

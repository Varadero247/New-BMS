**Bloom's level target**: Apply

## Section 1: Room Management

The physical environment directly affects learning. A trainer who manages the room well creates the conditions for engagement; one who ignores it fights against the room all day.

### Room Layout Options

| Configuration | Description | Best For |
|--------------|-------------|---------|
| **Horseshoe / U-shape** | Tables arranged in a U; trainer works inside the U | Discussion-heavy sessions; groups of 6–12 |
| **Cabaret / Clusters** | Small tables of 3–4; trainer circulates | Lab work; group activities; peer discussion |
| **Boardroom** | Single long table | Formal settings; management-level groups (less effective for learning) |
| **Theatre** | Rows of chairs; no tables | Large groups only; not appropriate for T3 (max 8 participants) |
| **Semicircle** | Chairs only, no tables | Short sessions; high-energy discussions; no writing required |

**Recommended for T3**: Horseshoe for modules; cabaret for labs. Convert between configurations at the lunch break if needed (takes 5–7 minutes with participant help — ask for volunteers).

### Managing the Room During Delivery

- **Stand up** when presenting; **sit down** when facilitating peer discussion — the physical shift signals the mode change
- **Use the full room** — do not anchor to the projection screen or your laptop; move to the far end of the horseshoe during discussion to draw quieter participants in
- **Make eye contact** across the whole group; do not default to "friends" (engaged participants) while ignoring "avoiders" (less engaged)
- **Seating**: If two participants are clearly acquainted and likely to talk to each other, position them on opposite sides of the U

### Managing Technical Issues

AV failure, Wi-Fi dropout, and sandbox unavailability are common. Responses:

| Issue | Response |
|-------|---------|
| Projector fails | Proceed verbally; use the whiteboard; do not stop for 15 minutes trying to fix AV |
| Sandbox is down | Switch to a pre-prepared screenshot walkthrough; acknowledge it; move on |
| Participant laptop cannot access sandbox | Pair them with an adjacent participant temporarily; resolve at break |
| Wi-Fi is slow | Use the trainer device for demonstrations; let participants join in at exercises only |

**Key principle**: Technical issues are your problem to solve, not the participants'. They should not be waiting while you debug. If resolution takes >3 minutes, continue without and address at break.

## Section 2: Questioning Techniques

Questions are the primary instrument of facilitation. A trainer who does not question is presenting, not facilitating. The four core questioning types for compliance training:

### Open Questions

**Definition**: Questions with no single correct answer; designed to elicit participant views, experience, and reasoning.

**Format**: Begin with *what, how, who, why, when, describe, tell me about*

**Examples**:
- "What challenges have you encountered when training new staff on compliance procedures?"
- "How does your organisation currently handle the gap between training completion and training effectiveness?"
- "Describe what a good induction looks like in your experience."

**When to use**: Session openings; discussion segments; after a new concept to check understanding at a deeper level than "any questions?"

**Common mistake**: Asking open questions and then filling the silence immediately. Wait. Adults process before responding. Wait five full seconds before rephrasing or redirecting. This feels longer than it is — time it in practice.

---

### Probing Questions

**Definition**: Follow-up questions that deepen or extend a participant's initial response.

**Format**: "Can you say more about…?", "What makes you say…?", "Can you give me an example of…?", "What happened next?", "How did that work out?"

**Examples**:
- Participant: "We find participants don't engage with compliance training." → Trainer: "Can you give me a specific example of that? What did it look like?"
- Participant: "The system is confusing." → Trainer: "Confusing in what way specifically? Is it the navigation, the terminology, something else?"

**When to use**: Whenever a participant gives a vague or general response; whenever you want to encourage them to think at a deeper level; whenever a response contains an assumption worth examining.

**Caution**: Probing can feel interrogative if overused or delivered with the wrong tone. Keep your body language open; nod; maintain a curious (not sceptical) expression.

---

### Hypothetical Questions

**Definition**: Questions that present a scenario and ask participants to reason through what they would do.

**Format**: "What would you do if…?", "Suppose you had a participant who…", "Imagine you were delivering this to…", "If this happened on Day 2 of your first cohort, how would you handle it?"

**Examples**:
- "What would you do if, during the written assessment, you discovered a participant had a completed answer sheet on their phone?"
- "Suppose your first cohort includes the manager of one of the participants — how would that affect your facilitation approach?"
- "If a participant challenges the Nexara system design itself rather than the training — how do you respond?"

**When to use**: During facilitation skills modules; when teaching assessment administration; when preparing participants for edge cases they will encounter in real delivery.

**Value**: Hypothetical questions prepare participants for scenarios they haven't yet faced. They reveal gaps in understanding that "any questions?" never surfaces.

---

### Rhetorical Questions

**Definition**: Questions that make a point rather than seeking an answer. The answer is implied; participants are not expected to respond.

**Format**: Typically closed or leading; follow with a pause; then make the point.

**Examples**:
- "Because if participants don't understand *why* the system exists, how can we expect them to use it consistently?" [pause] "That's why the opening five minutes of every session are critical."
- "Can you really assess learning if you've never seen the participant apply it?" [pause] "That's exactly why the delivery assessment exists."

**When to use**: To emphasise a key point; to create a moment of reflection; to set up a principle. Use sparingly — overuse makes delivery sound scripted.

---

### Question Sequencing — A Practical Model

Use this sequence for any discussion topic:

1. **Open** — broaden the field ("What comes to mind when you think about…?")
2. **Follow-up** — invite specific contributions ("Who can add to that?")
3. **Probing** — deepen a specific response ("You mentioned X — can you elaborate on what you mean by that?")
4. **Hypothetical** — apply to a scenario ("Given what [Name] described, what would you do if…?")
5. **Synthesise** — pull threads together ("So what we're hearing is… Does that capture it?")

## Section 3: Managing Difficult Participants

Every training cohort contains challenging dynamics. The following four archetypes appear frequently in compliance training contexts.

### Archetype 1: The Dominator

**Description**: Contributes constantly; answers every question before others can; sometimes redirects the session to their preferred topic.

**Risk**: Other participants disengage; session loses structure; quieter participants are never heard.

**Handling strategies**:
- Acknowledge their contribution positively, then redirect: "Great point. [Name], what's your reaction to that?"
- Use deliberate directed questions to quieter participants before the dominator can respond: "I'd like to hear from [Quiet Name] first — what do you think?"
- At a break: "Your contributions are really valuable — I want to make sure everyone gets a chance to engage too. Can I count on you to give others a beat before jumping in?"
- Seat the dominator next to you rather than across the room — proximity reduces outbursts; distance increases them.

**Do not**: Silence them publicly ("Let's give someone else a chance" said to the room while looking at them); this creates resentment and the rest of the group feels uncomfortable.

---

### Archetype 2: The Disengaged

**Description**: Minimal verbal contribution; may be on their phone; appears to be elsewhere mentally; gives monosyllabic responses when called on.

**Risk**: Contagion — disengagement is visible and can spread; also the disengaged participant is not learning and may fail the assessment.

**Handling strategies**:
- Direct a low-stakes question to them: "I'm curious what you think, [Name] — what's your experience of compliance training before today?"
- If they are on their phone during an activity: move physically close to them; proximity is a gentle prompt
- At a break (private): "I get the sense today might not be hitting where you need it. What would make it more useful for you?"
- Pair them with an engaged participant for lab work — peer engagement is more effective than trainer prompting

**Do not**: Call them out publicly for being on their phone; this creates defensiveness and ruins the psychological contract.

---

### Archetype 3: The Sceptic

**Description**: Questions the training design, the Nexara system, the organisation's decision to implement the system, or the value of certification.

**Risk**: If not handled well, their scepticism validates disengagement in other participants.

**Handling strategies**:
- **Do not dismiss**: "That's a fair challenge. Let me address it directly." Sceptics who are taken seriously often become engaged advocates.
- Acknowledge legitimate concerns: "I hear that. Nexara isn't perfect for every workflow — are there specific features that aren't working for your team?"
- Separate product feedback from training feedback: "If you have specific feedback on the system, I can make sure that reaches the Nexara product team. Today we're working with what's live — let's make sure you can use it as effectively as possible."
- If the scepticism is about the training approach: "What would make this more useful to you? I'm open to adjusting."

**Do not**: Argue with a sceptic about whether compliance is important; this never resolves and wastes group time.

---

### Archetype 4: The Over-Eager

**Description**: Enthusiastic, motivated, fast; completes exercises early, asks extension questions, shares multiple examples.

**Risk**: Can inadvertently embarrass slower participants; may pressure the trainer to skip content or move faster.

**Handling strategies**:
- Use them as a peer resource: "Since you've finished, could you help [Name] with step 3?"
- Give extension tasks from the lab files (each lab contains an extension task for this reason)
- Acknowledge them while maintaining pace: "Fantastic — I want to make sure everyone's with us before we move on."
- Avoid singling them out as "the best" — this creates competitive rather than collaborative group dynamics

**Do not**: Slow them down artificially or ignore them to maintain pace — bored eager participants create noise.

## Section 4: Energy Management

### Reading Energy Levels

Experienced facilitators develop an ability to read group energy in real time. Observable indicators:

| Energy Level | Signs | Response |
|-------------|-------|---------|
| **High** | Animated discussion; forward posture; spontaneous contributions | Let it run; don't interrupt flow with structured activities |
| **Moderate** | Engagement on cue; responding to questions; staying with content | Normal delivery; maintain current pace |
| **Low** | Back in chairs; minimal eye contact; mono-syllabic responses; side conversations | Shift mode immediately; do not continue lecture |
| **Crashed** | Physical movement (stretching, yawning); phone-checking; conversational "hum" | Movement break, even unscheduled; shift to physical activity |

### Energy Management Techniques

**Pace variation**: Slow down to emphasise a critical point; speed up slightly for well-understood content. Monotone pace kills energy regardless of content quality.

**Physical movement**: At 60-minute intervals, build in a reason to move: "Before we continue, stand up and find one other person in the room to discuss this with for two minutes." Standing discussion circuits restore blood flow and attention.

**Activity switching**: Alternate between trainer-led segments (lecture, demonstration) and participant-led segments (pair work, sandbox exercise) every 20–30 minutes. Never present for 60 consecutive minutes without an interactive element.

**The energiser**: For severe energy crashes, use a short competitive or creative task. "60 seconds. Tell your neighbour the most important thing you've learned this morning. Go." Urgency creates energy.

**Silence as a tool**: Counterintuitively, planned silence (asking a question and genuinely waiting) creates alertness. Participants become aware that a response is expected; they cannot coast during silence.

### Energy Patterns Through the Day

| Time | Typical Energy | Trainer Strategy |
|------|---------------|-----------------|
| 08:30–09:30 | Rising; engagement building | Set an inclusive, energetic tone |
| 09:30–10:30 | Moderate; sustained | Maintain pace; use Q&A to sustain |
| 10:30 | Break | Trainer recharges too |
| 10:45–12:00 | Good post-break | Use this window for skills practice |
| 12:00–12:15 | Pre-lunch decline | Wind down; don't introduce new concepts |
| 13:00–13:30 | Post-lunch dip | Interactive activity immediately; no lecture |
| 13:30–14:30 | Recovery | Lab work; sandbox exercises |
| 14:30–15:45 | Micro-teaching lab; naturally high | Leverage engagement |
| 15:45–16:30 | Reflective fatigue | Debrief; not new content |
| 16:30–17:00 | End-of-day energy | Keep formative check short; close with energy |

## Section 5: Opening a Session

The first five minutes of any training session are the most important. They determine whether participants engage or coast. A well-structured opening contains four elements:

### Element 1: Welcome and Context (60 seconds)
State your name, your role, why you're qualified to deliver this content, and what the session will achieve. Avoid excessive preamble. Do not open with "So…" or "Basically…" — they signal unpreparedness.

### Element 2: The Hook (90 seconds)
A relevant story, striking statistic, or provocative question that creates immediate interest. Example for End User Training: "Last year, a manufacturing company in the same sector as yours had a significant incident — the investigation found that a near-miss with the same root cause had occurred eight months earlier. It wasn't recorded in their system. Today we're going to make sure that never happens to your organisation."

### Element 3: Agenda and Expectations (60 seconds)
Show the session agenda. Briefly state what participants will be able to do by the end. Mention any housekeeping (breaks, fire exits, devices). Do not read the agenda aloud line by line — participants can read.

### Element 4: Activate Prior Knowledge (2 minutes)
Ask a question that connects to participants' existing experience. "Before we start: what's your current process for recording incidents? Shout out two words — what does it look like today?" This creates relevance and gives you data about the group's starting point.

## Section 1: Andragogy — The Four Principles of Adult Learning

Malcolm Knowles coined the term *andragogy* to describe how adults learn differently from children. His four core principles — sometimes listed as six in later editions — are the foundation for effective compliance training design. As a Nexara certified internal trainer, you will apply these principles every time you design or deliver a session.

### Principle 1: Adults Need to Know Why

Adults do not accept instruction passively. Before a new skill or concept is meaningful, adults require a convincing answer to: "Why does this matter to me, specifically, in my role?"

**Application in Nexara training**: Before demonstrating the Incident Management module, say: "When an incident is recorded correctly, your organisation can prove to a regulator that the event was identified, investigated, and corrected. If it's missing from the system, it never happened — legally." That is a "why" that lands.

**Contrast**: "Today we will cover the Incident Management module" — provides no motivation to engage.

**Common mistake**: Skipping the "why" because the trainer finds the content self-evidently important. Subject-matter experts consistently over-estimate how much their participants already care about a topic.

---

### Principle 2: Adults Bring Experience to Learning

Adult learners do not arrive as blank slates. They have professional experience, mental models, and established habits — all of which affect how they receive new information. Effective trainers *build on* existing experience rather than ignoring it.

**Application**: When introducing the Permit to Work module, ask: "Who currently uses a paper PTW system? What works about it? What doesn't?" This activates existing knowledge, creates readiness for the digital solution, and immediately shows participants that their experience is valued.

**Contrast**: Beginning with "The Permit to Work module allows you to…" treats participants as having no relevant prior knowledge.

**Important nuance**: Existing experience can also create *resistance*. Participants who have used a different HSEQ system for ten years may resist Nexara because it works differently. Acknowledge this directly: "You've built good habits with your current system. Today we're building new habits for this one. Some things will feel wrong at first — that's normal."

---

### Principle 3: Adults Are Ready to Learn When They Need To

Adult learning is *triggered by relevance*. Unlike children who learn for future use, adults learn best when the content addresses a current need or immediate application. A compliance training session that happens six months before a system goes live has a retention disadvantage.

**Application**: Schedule End User training as close to system go-live as practical. For Module Owner training, schedule immediately after the module is activated for that team. "Just-in-time" delivery significantly outperforms "ahead-of-time" delivery for adult learners.

**For T3 trainers specifically**: When you are planning your first cohort, schedule it within two weeks of any system update that affects the modules you'll be teaching. Relevance is timing as much as content.

---

### Principle 4: Adults Are Problem-Centred, Not Subject-Centred

Adults engage more deeply with training when it is structured around real problems they need to solve, rather than around subject matter for its own sake. Compliance training that is organised by module feature (here is what each button does) is less effective than training organised by task (here is how you record an incident from the moment it happens to the point where it is closed).

**Application**: Frame every Nexara training module as a workflow, not a feature tour. End User training follows the participant's actual working day: they observe an event, they raise an incident, they acknowledge a training record, they check their permit status. The *task* drives the navigation, not the menu structure.

**Worked example**: Compare these two structures for a 45-minute Environmental Aspects session:
- *Subject-centred*: "We'll cover the Aspects screen, the Significance Score formula, the Event linkage, and the Legal Register."
- *Problem-centred*: "You need to demonstrate that your organisation is managing its most significant environmental impacts. Let's walk through how to identify, score, and action them — and how to prove it to an auditor."

The second structure is objectively more engaging for adult learners. Use it.

## Section 2: Bloom's Taxonomy — Targeting the Right Cognitive Level

Bloom's Taxonomy describes six levels of cognitive skill, from simple recall at the base to synthesis and creation at the top:

| Level | Verb Examples | Compliance Training Application |
|-------|--------------|--------------------------------|
| 1. Remember | Recall, identify, list | "What is the NC severity scale?" |
| 2. Understand | Explain, describe, summarise | "Explain why containment actions differ from corrective actions" |
| 3. Apply | Demonstrate, use, execute | "Raise an NC record for this scenario" |
| 4. Analyse | Differentiate, classify, compare | "Analyse these two CAPA records — which has the stronger root cause?" |
| 5. Evaluate | Judge, critique, justify | "Evaluate whether this CAPA should be marked Effective" |
| 6. Create | Design, construct, produce | "Design a training schedule for a new Module Owner cohort" |

### Target Level for Nexara Training

- **End User Training**: Target Level 3 (Apply). Participants must be able to *perform* tasks in the system, not just recall where things are.
- **Module Owner Training**: Target Levels 3–4 (Apply + Analyse). Module Owners must make judgements about record categorisation, severity, significance scoring — not just enter data.
- **Train-the-Trainer**: Targets Levels 3–6 (Apply → Create). You are being asked to design and deliver training, which requires the full cognitive range.

### Mapping Assessment Questions

Well-designed MCQ questions should test at the appropriate Bloom's level. A question that asks "Where is the NC module in the navigation menu?" tests Level 1 (Remember). A question that asks "An inspector finds that the NC for this event was closed without a CAPA — what is the consequence for ISO 9001 compliance?" tests Level 4 (Analyse).

**Common mistake**: Writing assessment questions that are all at Level 1 — producing participants who can recall facts but cannot perform under real conditions.

## Section 3: Learning Styles — Accommodating Multiple Modalities

The VARK model (Neil Fleming) categorises learning style preferences as:

| Mode | Description | Training Accommodation |
|------|-------------|----------------------|
| **V — Visual** | Preference for diagrams, charts, spatial information | Workflow diagrams, process maps, dashboard screenshots |
| **A — Auditory** | Preference for spoken explanation, discussion | Verbal explanation, Q&A, group discussion |
| **R — Reading/Writing** | Preference for text-based information | Handouts, reference guides, written exercises |
| **K — Kinaesthetic** | Preference for doing, practice, tactile experience | Sandbox exercises, role-play scenarios, case studies |

### Practical Application

Every Nexara training session should contain elements of all four modes:

1. **Visual**: Show the module in context — a completed record, a populated dashboard, a process flowchart
2. **Auditory**: Explain the "why" verbally; facilitate discussion; use your voice intentionally (vary pace, volume, emphasis)
3. **Reading/Writing**: Provide the Nexara module handout; use the written self-assessment at session end
4. **Kinaesthetic**: Make participants raise a record, run a workflow, or complete a scenario in the sandbox

**Important nuance**: The research on VARK as a fixed individual characteristic is contested. What is not contested: varying instructional modality within a session significantly improves retention for the group as a whole. Do not obsess over identifying each participant's "type" — instead, build all four into your session design.

## Section 4: Cognitive Load Theory — Designing Training That Sticks

John Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory identifies three types of mental effort involved in learning:

| Load Type | Source | Trainer Action |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| **Intrinsic** | The inherent complexity of the content itself | Sequence content from simple to complex; scaffold before removing supports |
| **Extraneous** | Complexity *added by the instructor* — cluttered slides, jargon, tangents | Simplify materials; use labels not database names; stay on topic |
| **Germane** | The productive mental effort of actually learning | Protect this by reducing the other two; use practice, retrieval, reflection |

### Reducing Extraneous Load in Nexara Training

Specific extraneous load risks in IMS training (and how to reduce them):

- **Using database column names** instead of UI labels (e.g. "the `date_occurred` field" vs. "the Date of Occurrence field") → *always use UI labels*
- **Showing all features of a module at once** before participants have practised the core workflow → *sequence: core task first, advanced features later*
- **Dense slides with 8+ bullet points** → *one key point per slide; use screenshots of actual Nexara screens*
- **Simultaneous explanation + demonstration** without allowing time to do both → *explain first, demonstrate second, practice third*
- **Module content delivered in system menu order** (e.g. top to bottom of the sidebar) rather than task-flow order → *always structure by workflow, not UI layout*

## Section 5: Compliance Training Psychology

### The Box-Ticking Effect

Research in compliance training consistently identifies a phenomenon called the "box-ticking" effect: participants disengage from compliance training when they perceive it as a bureaucratic requirement rather than a genuine learning opportunity. The consequences:

- Low retention (participants forget 70% of content within 24 hours)
- Low transfer (participants cannot apply content when the real situation arises)
- Resentment (mandatory compliance training is associated with distrust and control)

### Breaking the Box-Ticking Pattern

As a Nexara internal trainer, you are likely to encounter participants who arrive with box-ticking expectations — particularly for End User training. The following strategies reliably disrupt this pattern:

**1. Open with a story, not an agenda**
Begin with a real or plausible scenario — an incident that occurred because someone didn't know how to use the system, or a near-miss that was caught because someone did. Make it contextually relevant to the participant's industry.

**2. Acknowledge the mandatory nature early**
"Yes, this is mandatory. Here's why your organisation made it mandatory — and why I think, after today, you'll understand it was the right call." Acknowledging the reality reduces defensiveness.

**3. Make competence visible quickly**
Within the first 20 minutes, give participants a small sandbox task they can complete successfully. Early success creates investment. Participants who have successfully raised an incident in the system feel ownership; they are no longer passive recipients.

**4. Connect to personal consequences**
"If an incident isn't recorded in this system, and something happens later with the same root cause — and there's an investigation — the question will be: why wasn't the first event recorded?" Personal relevance beats abstract compliance rhetoric every time.

**5. Use a closing reflection**
At session end: "Write down one thing that happened today that you will do differently next week." This simple act increases transfer rates significantly. It does not matter if participants struggle to answer — the act of trying activates retrieval and intent.

# In-Person Delivery Guide — Nexara Training Programmes

**Programme**: Nexara IMS Train-the-Trainer (T3) and all downstream programmes
**Version**: 1.0

---

## 1. Room Requirements

### Minimum Room Specification

| Requirement | Specification |
|-------------|-------------|
| Capacity | Minimum 50% more than participant count (e.g. 12 participants → 18-person room) |
| Layout flexibility | Furniture must be moveable — fixed theatre or lecture hall seating is not suitable |
| Projection or display | Minimum 65" screen or standard projector; visible from all positions |
| Natural lighting | Preferred; blackout capability required for projector use in bright rooms |
| Internet | Reliable Wi-Fi with ≥ 10 Mbps; confirm network name and password in advance |
| Catering access | Access to tea/coffee facilities within 2 minutes for breaks |
| Accessibility | Compliant with your local accessibility requirements (wheelchair access, hearing loop if applicable) |
| Separate breakout space | Preferred but not required — corridor space or a secondary room for 1:1 conversations during assessments |

### Recommended Room Layouts

**Horseshoe / U-shape** (preferred for T3 and Module Owner Training)

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────┐
│           [PROJECTION SCREEN]            │
│                                          │
│   ┌─────┐               ┌─────┐         │
│   │  P  │               │  P  │         │
│   └─────┘               └─────┘         │
│                                          │
│   ┌─────┐   [TRAINER]   ┌─────┐         │
│   │  P  │               │  P  │         │
│   └─────┘               └─────┘         │
│                                          │
│   ┌─────┐               ┌─────┐         │
│   │  P  │               │  P  │         │
│   └─────┘               └─────┘         │
└──────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Benefits**: Every participant can see both the trainer and each other; trainer can move within the U; good for discussion and observation.

**Cabaret / Cluster** (preferred for lab sessions)

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────┐
│           [PROJECTION SCREEN]            │
│                                          │
│  ┌──────┐     ┌──────┐     ┌──────┐     │
│  │P1  P2│     │P3  P4│     │P5  P6│     │
│  └──────┘     └──────┘     └──────┘     │
│                                          │
│         ┌──────┐     ┌──────┐           │
│         │P7  P8│     │      │           │
│         └──────┘     └──────┘           │
└──────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Benefits**: Natural peer collaboration; easier for sandbox exercises; can pair participants strategically.

**Converting between layouts**: Ask for volunteer help at breaks; conversion takes 5–7 minutes. Plan the conversion in your session schedule.

## 4. Sandbox Access During In-Person Delivery

### Trainer Demonstration Mode

For the trainer's demonstration: connect to the room display and demonstrate in the sandbox using the trainer device. Participants follow along on their own devices.

### Participant Sandbox Access

All participants access the sandbox on their own devices (laptop or tablet preferred). Distribute credentials at the start of the sandbox exercise (not at the session start — reduces distraction during non-sandbox segments).

**Credential distribution method**: Write participant credentials on a folded tent card at each seat, face down. Ask participants to turn them over at the start of the first sandbox exercise.

**Common participant sandbox issues**:

| Problem | Solution |
|---------|---------|
| "It's not loading" | Check Wi-Fi connection; try a different browser |
| "I can see it but can't click anything" | They may be logged in with a read-only role; check their assigned role in the admin console |
| "My data from the exercise isn't saving" | Sandbox saves on submission/record creation; remind them to click "Save" or "Submit" |
| "I accidentally deleted something" | Minor resets within the sandbox exercise are normal; continue with what's there |
| Two participants get the same login | This creates conflicts; assign each participant a unique demo login (demo1 through demo8) |

## 5. Physical Energy Management

Physical presence gives you more energy management tools than virtual delivery. Use them:

| Technique | How |
|-----------|-----|
| **Move around the room** | Do not stand in one spot for more than 10 minutes; walk to different parts of the U; stand near the back when facilitating discussion |
| **Use the whiteboard actively** | Write key terms, draw diagrams, map participant responses — activity at the whiteboard is visually engaging |
| **Stand up, sit down signals** | Stand when presenting/demonstrating; sit at the same level as participants when facilitating discussion — the physical shift signals mode |
| **Group movements** | "For this exercise, please move your chair to face your neighbour." Physical movement resets energy even if brief |
| **Outdoor break** | If the venue allows, encourage participants to step outside for morning and afternoon breaks. Natural light and movement are the best energy resets |

## 6. Contingency Plans

### AV Failure

| Scenario | Plan |
|----------|------|
| Projector fails | Use the room's whiteboard for key points; deliver verbally; use the sandbox on participants' own devices for demonstrations |
| Laptop fails | Use backup screenshots on a USB drive opened on another device; or use a participant's laptop as the demonstration device |
| Projector and laptop both fail | Proceed verbally with participant discussion and sandbox on own devices; deprioritise visual content; focus on sandbox exercises and discussion |

### Participant Access Issues

| Scenario | Plan |
|----------|------|
| 1–2 participants cannot access sandbox | Pair them with an adjacent participant; resolve during a break |
| Majority cannot access sandbox | Pivot to discussion and whiteboard-based delivery for the affected segment; re-run sandbox exercises if access is restored |
| Assessment portal is down (Day 2) | Switch to printed assessment papers (keep 2 spare copies as standard contingency); mark manually; enter results in portal when restored |

### Participant Drop-Out (Day 2 Assessment Day)

| Scenario | Plan |
|----------|------|
| Participant cannot attend Day 2 delivery assessment | Cannot complete assessment today; schedule a makeup delivery assessment within 30 days; written assessment may still be taken if they can join for 15:15 window |
| Participant becomes ill during Day 2 | If before their delivery slot: note for rescheduling; written assessment can be taken separately if they remain. If during written assessment: note the time stopped; a new assessment attempt is needed |

## 7. Catering Recommendations

| Item | Notes |
|------|-------|
| Morning tea/coffee | Available from 08:15; refreshed at morning break |
| Lunch | Hot or substantial cold options; avoid very heavy meals (reduce post-lunch energy crash) |
| Afternoon break | Tea/coffee + light snacks; something with protein or complex carbs (avoids the sugar crash) |
| Water | Always available on tables throughout the day |
| Dietary requirements | Collect at enrolment; confirm with catering 3 days before |

**Post-lunch energy tip**: Schedule the most interactive, hands-on content immediately after lunch (typically the lab or sandbox exercise) — not a lecture. If your session structure requires a lecture post-lunch, build in a 3-minute standing discussion task at the 13:15 mark.
